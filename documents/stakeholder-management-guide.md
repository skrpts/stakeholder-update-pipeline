## Stakeholder Management Guide

### Why Stakeholder Management Matters

Product managers who neglect stakeholder management eventually find their best work undermined by misalignment, surprise, or political resistance. Stakeholder management is not about politics — it is about ensuring the people who can help or hinder your product's success are informed, aligned, and engaged at the right level.

### Stakeholder Mapping

#### Step 1: Identify Your Stakeholders

List everyone who has influence over, interest in, or is affected by your product. Common stakeholder groups:

- **Decision-makers:** Those who approve budgets, strategies, or go/no-go decisions (executives, board members)
- **Influencers:** Those who shape opinions but may not have formal authority (senior engineers, key customers, industry analysts)
- **Contributors:** Those who provide resources, skills, or capabilities (engineering teams, design, marketing)
- **Beneficiaries:** Those who use or are affected by the product (end users, customer success, support teams)
- **Gatekeepers:** Those who control access to resources or information (legal, compliance, security, procurement)

#### Step 2: Assess Power and Interest

For each stakeholder, assess:

- **Power:** How much ability do they have to affect the product's success? (High/Medium/Low)
- **Interest:** How much do they care about the product's direction and outcomes? (High/Medium/Low)

This produces four quadrants:

| | High Interest | Low Interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | Manage closely — regular updates, seek input, align expectations | Keep satisfied — provide key information, respond to concerns promptly |
| **Low Power** | Keep informed — regular updates, answer questions, acknowledge contributions | Monitor — light-touch communication, available if interest increases |

#### Step 3: Define Engagement Strategy

For each high-power or high-interest stakeholder:

- **Communication cadence:** How often do they need to hear from you?
- **Preferred channel:** Do they prefer email, meetings, Slack, or formal documents?
- **Information depth:** Do they want strategic summaries or operational detail?
- **Decision involvement:** What decisions should they be involved in, and at what stage?
- **Concerns and motivations:** What are they most worried about? What outcomes do they care about most?

### Managing Expectations

#### Setting Expectations

- **Be explicit about what you will deliver and when.** Vague commitments ("we're working on it") create misalignment.
- **Underpromise, then deliver.** It is always better to exceed expectations than to miss them.
- **Communicate constraints.** If resources, timeline, or scope are limited, say so upfront rather than discovering it mid-project.
- **Agree on success criteria.** Before a project begins, align with key stakeholders on how success will be measured.

#### Managing Expectation Gaps

When reality diverges from expectations:

1. **Detect early.** Monitor leading indicators (velocity, burn rate, blocker count) that signal the plan is at risk.
2. **Communicate proactively.** Stakeholders who hear about problems from you first are far more receptive than those who discover them independently.
3. **Present options, not just problems.** "We are behind schedule. We can cut scope, extend the timeline, or add resources. Here is my recommendation and why."
4. **Negotiate formally.** If the ask has changed (more scope, fewer resources, tighter timeline), renegotiate the commitment explicitly. Do not silently absorb unrealistic expectations.

### Difficult Stakeholder Situations

#### The Absent Stakeholder
A key decision-maker who is unresponsive or unavailable.
**Approach:** Establish a deputy. Document decisions and share them with a "silence is consent" framing: "I plan to proceed with Option A unless I hear otherwise by Friday."

#### The Micromanager
A stakeholder who wants to be involved in every detail.
**Approach:** Increase communication frequency to reduce anxiety. Provide operational detail proactively so they do not need to chase it. Agree on explicit boundaries: "I will update you weekly on progress and escalate any scope or timeline changes immediately."

#### The Scope Creep Champion
A stakeholder who continuously adds requirements without acknowledging trade-offs.
**Approach:** Make trade-offs visible. "We can add Feature X, but it means deferring Feature Y by two weeks. Which do you prefer?" Never silently absorb new scope.

#### Conflicting Stakeholders
Two or more stakeholders with incompatible requirements or priorities.
**Approach:** Surface the conflict explicitly. Bring conflicting stakeholders into the same room (or thread) and facilitate a decision. Do not try to satisfy both through ambiguity — it always fails later.

### Building Trust

Trust is the currency of stakeholder management. It is built through:

- **Reliability:** Do what you said you would do, when you said you would do it
- **Transparency:** Share information proactively, including bad news
- **Competence:** Demonstrate understanding of the domain, the data, and the trade-offs
- **Candour:** Give honest assessments even when they are uncomfortable
- **Follow-through:** Close the loop on every commitment, question, and concern raised
