## Company OKRs

This source document provides a reference framework for company-level objectives and key results. Product workflows reference this to ensure initiatives align with organisational goals.

### OKR Structure

**Objectives** are qualitative descriptions of what the company wants to achieve. Good objectives are:
- Inspiring and ambitious (stretch beyond current trajectory)
- Specific enough to guide prioritisation
- Time-bound (typically quarterly or annual)
- Limited in number (3-5 per quarter)

**Key Results** are quantitative measures of progress toward an objective. Good key results are:
- Measurable with a specific number and deadline
- Outcome-focused (measure results, not activities)
- Challenging but achievable (70% achievement is typical for stretch goals)
- Limited to 2-4 per objective

### Example OKR Framework

**Objective 1: Accelerate user growth**
- KR1: Increase monthly active users from X to Y by end of quarter
- KR2: Improve onboarding completion rate from X% to Y%
- KR3: Reduce time-to-first-value from X days to Y days

**Objective 2: Deepen user engagement**
- KR1: Increase average session duration from X to Y minutes
- KR2: Grow weekly active users as a percentage of monthly active users from X% to Y%
- KR3: Increase feature adoption breadth (users engaging with 3+ features) from X% to Y%

**Objective 3: Build sustainable revenue**
- KR1: Grow ARR from £Xm to £Ym
- KR2: Reduce churn rate from X% to Y% monthly
- KR3: Increase average revenue per user from £X to £Y

**Objective 4: Strengthen operational excellence**
- KR1: Achieve 99.9% uptime (from current 99.X%)
- KR2: Reduce mean time to resolution for P1 incidents from X hours to Y hours
- KR3: Ship X% of roadmap items on or ahead of schedule

### Using OKRs in Product Workflows

When evaluating product initiatives:

1. **Map to OKRs:** Every proposed initiative should connect to at least one company OKR. If it does not, either the initiative is misaligned or the OKRs are incomplete.

2. **Quantify contribution:** Estimate how much the initiative contributes to the relevant key result. "This feature is expected to improve onboarding completion by 5 percentage points, contributing to KR2 of Objective 1."

3. **Prioritise by impact:** When competing initiatives map to the same OKR, prioritise by expected contribution to the key result relative to effort.

4. **Flag conflicts:** When an initiative advances one OKR but harms another (e.g., a growth feature that increases support load), surface the trade-off explicitly.

### OKR Anti-Patterns

- **Activity-based KRs:** "Launch feature X" is an output, not an outcome. Measure the impact of launching feature X instead.
- **Sandbagged targets:** KRs that the team knows they will hit without effort do not drive alignment or ambition.
- **Too many OKRs:** More than 5 objectives or 4 KRs per objective dilutes focus.
- **No owner:** Every KR needs a named owner responsible for tracking and reporting progress.
- **Set and forget:** OKRs must be reviewed at least monthly. If a KR is off track, adjust the approach or escalate.
