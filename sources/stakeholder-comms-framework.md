## Stakeholder Communications Framework

### Communication Matrix

| Stakeholder Group | Cadence | Channel | Depth | Primary Focus | Owner |
|-------------------|---------|---------|-------|---------------|-------|
| Board of Directors | Quarterly | Deck + meeting | Strategic | Business outcomes, market position, financials | CPO / CEO |
| Executive Leadership | Bi-weekly or monthly | Email + brief | Moderate | Goal attainment, risks, resource needs | Product VP |
| Cross-Functional Partners | Weekly or bi-weekly | Email + Slack | Operational | Dependencies, shared milestones, collaboration | Product Manager |
| Direct Team | Weekly | Stand-up + written update | Detailed | Execution, blockers, recognition | Product Manager / Team Lead |
| Customers | Monthly or quarterly | Newsletter / release notes | Curated | New features, improvements, upcoming changes | Product Marketing |
| Investors | Quarterly | Deck + call | Strategic | Growth, unit economics, market opportunity | CEO / CFO |

### Communication Principles

#### 1. Audience-First Design

Every communication should be designed for its audience, not for the convenience of the sender. This means:

- **Content selection:** Include only information relevant to the audience's role and decision authority
- **Depth calibration:** Executives need summaries; teams need detail; partners need actionable specifics
- **Tone matching:** Board communications are formal and strategic; team communications are operational and appreciative
- **Jargon management:** Technical terms in team updates; business terms in executive updates; plain language for customers

#### 2. The Pyramid Principle

Structure all stakeholder communications using Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle:

1. **Start with the answer:** Lead with the conclusion, recommendation, or headline finding
2. **Group supporting arguments:** Organise evidence into logical groups (typically 3-5)
3. **Order logically:** Present groups in order of importance, chronology, or dependency
4. **Details last:** Place supporting data and methodology at the end, available for those who want it

This structure respects the reader's time and ensures the key message is received even if the reader stops halfway through.

#### 3. Cadence Discipline

Consistent cadence builds trust and reduces ad-hoc information requests:

- **Never skip a scheduled update** — even a brief "no significant changes this period" maintains the cadence and signals reliability
- **Front-load bad news** — if something is going wrong, communicate it at the next scheduled touchpoint rather than waiting until it is a crisis
- **Separate status updates from decision requests** — do not bury an ask inside a routine update; make decision requests explicit and standalone when urgency warrants it

#### 4. Feedback Loops

Stakeholder communication is not one-directional:

- **Track engagement:** Do stakeholders read the updates? Do they respond? Lack of engagement may indicate the content is not useful.
- **Solicit feedback periodically:** Ask stakeholders whether the updates are the right frequency, depth, and focus
- **Close the loop on decisions:** When a stakeholder makes a decision based on your update, acknowledge it and report on the outcome

### Common Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Remedy |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Information dump | Sending all available data without curation | Apply the Pyramid Principle; lead with what matters |
| Spin | Presenting only positive information | Include risks and challenges alongside wins |
| Asymmetric transparency | Sharing different facts with different audiences | Keep substance consistent; adapt depth and framing |
| Update fatigue | Sending too many updates too frequently | Consolidate into the agreed cadence; use a single channel |
| Decision avoidance | Presenting options without a recommendation | Always include a recommendation with your analysis |
| Retrospective only | Reporting only on what happened, not what comes next | Always include a forward-looking section |

### Templates and Formats

- Executive summaries: 300-500 words, bullet-heavy, one page maximum
- Board deck content: 8-12 slides, one key message per slide
- Stakeholder emails: 200-400 words per audience variant
- Team updates: 300-600 words, include blockers and recognition sections
- Customer communications: 150-300 words, focus on user benefit not internal process
